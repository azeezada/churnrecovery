## Posting Tips (Apply to All Threads)

**Best times to post:**
- Tuesday–Thursday, 9–11 AM EST (peak B2B engagement)
- Tuesday–Thursday, 7–9 PM EST (founder/indie hacker crowd)
- Avoid Mondays before noon and Fridays after 2 PM

**Engagement tactics:**
- Reply to comments within the first 30 minutes (Twitter algo rewards this)
- Don't drop the link until tweet 3+ (first tweet gets less reach if link is in hook)
- End with a question on the last tweet to drive replies
- Retweet your own thread 6–8 hours after posting with a short summary

**Hashtags (use sparingly — 1–2 max per thread):**
- `#buildinpublic` — for founder-voice / BIP threads
- `#SaaS` — for business/product threads
- `#IndieHacker` or `#indiehackers` — for the IH audience
- `#contentcreators` — for newsletter/creator threads
- Skip hashtags on threads 1, 2, 4 — they perform better without

## Thread 3: "I Analyzed 20 Subscription Business Cancel Flows"

**Target audience:** Subscription founders, SaaS operators, newsletter creators
**Goal:** Drive to /demo
**Tone:** Analytical, data-driven but accessible — teach first, sell later
**Best hashtags:** #SaaS (optional)

---

**1/**
Spent a week cancelling subscriptions to study their flows.

20 products. From $10/mo newsletters to $200/mo SaaS tools.

The difference between a 25% save rate and a 2% save rate came down to 4 patterns.

Here's what I found: 🧵

*(~233 chars)*

---

**2/**
First: most cancel flows are terrible.

12 of the 20 had:
- No offer
- No friction
- No attempt to understand why I was leaving

They just let me go.

One click, cancelled, done.

Those are the 2% save-rate businesses.

*(~217 chars)*

---

**3/**
Pattern 1: The Pause Offer

3 businesses offered to pause my subscription instead of cancel.

"Take a break for 30 days — we'll be here when you're ready."

This is the best tool for impulse cancellations (which represent ~40% of all churns).

*(~241 chars)*

---

**4/**
The pause offer works because most cancellations aren't permanent decisions.

Someone got busy.
Their budget got tight for a month.
They lost the habit.

Giving them a guilt-free "pause" removes the pressure.

30% of paused accounts return within 60 days.

*(~254 chars)*

---

**5/**
Pattern 2: The Discount Offer

6 businesses offered a discount when I tried to cancel.

"Stay for $5/month for the next 3 months."

This works — but it has a cost.

It trains users to cancel in order to get a deal. And it permanently hurts LTV.

*(~244 chars)*

---

**6/**
Use discounts carefully.

Good for: price objection cancellations ("it's too expensive")
Bad for: habitual cancellers who know the trick

The exit survey question matters here.
If they say "price," offer a discount.
If they say "too busy," offer a pause.

*(~249 chars)*

---

**7/**
Pattern 3: Exit Survey Only

4 businesses showed me a "Why are you leaving?" form.

No offer. Just data collection.

Useful for product teams. Zero save rate.

This is the laziest possible cancel flow. It helps you — not the customer.

*(~233 chars)*

---

**8/**
Pattern 4: Personalized Outreach

2 businesses (both high-ticket) sent a personal email after I cancelled.

"Hey, I noticed you left. Was there anything I could have done differently?"

This had the highest emotional resonance. Also the hardest to scale.

*(~253 chars)*

---

**9/**
So what actually works?

Best by save rate:
1. Personalized outreach (~30%+, but doesn't scale)
2. Smart pause + discount combo (~20–25%)
3. Pause only (~15–20%)
4. Discount only (~10–15%)
5. Exit survey only (~0%)
6. No cancel flow (~0%)

*(~242 chars)*

---

**10/**
The pattern that surprised me most:

The 2 businesses with 25%+ save rates both asked ONE question before making the offer.

"Why are you leaving?"

The answer determined the offer. Personalized retention is 2x more effective than generic.

Want to see what a good flow looks like? → https://churnrecovery.com/demo
